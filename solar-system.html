Planets & Celestial Bodies

Sun

The Sun is the heart of our solar system - a massive ball of burning hydrogen and helium that provides all the energy necessary for life on Earth. Its powerful gravity holds everything in orbit, from tiny Mercury to distant Neptune.

Type: G-type main-sequence star
Diameter: 1,391,000 km
Mass: 1.989 x 10^30 kg
Surface Temp: 5,500 °C
Orbits completed in given time: N/A

Mercury

Mercury is the smallest planet and the closest to the Sun. It zips around the Sun in just 88 Earth days - the fastest orbit of any planet. Its rocky, cratered surface is covered in extreme temperature swings, dropping to -180°C at night and soaring to 430°C during the day.

Diameter: 4,879 km
Distance from Sun: 57.9 million km
Orbital Period: 88 days (0.241 yr)
Day Length: 58.6 Earth days
Moons: 0
Orbits completed in given time: 0

Venus

Venus is often called Earth's "sister planet" because of their similar size, but it could not be more different. A runaway greenhouse effect has made it the hottest planet in the solar system - even hotter than Mercury - with surface temperatures reaching 465°C. Its thick carbon-dioxide atmosphere produces crushing pressure at the surface.

Diameter: 12,104 km
Distance from Sun: 108.2 million km
Orbital Period: 225 days (0.615 yr)
Day Length: 243 Earth days
Moons: 0
Orbits completed in given time: 0

Earth

Earth is the third planet from the Sun and the only known world to harbor life. About 71% of its surface is covered by water, earning it the nickname "the Blue Marble." Its atmosphere, magnetic field, and distance from the Sun create the perfect conditions for diverse ecosystems to thrive.

Diameter: 12,756 km
Distance from Sun: 149.6 million km
Orbital Period: 365.25 days (1 yr)
Day Length: 24 hours
Moons: 1
Orbits completed in given time: 0

Moon

Earth's only natural satellite, the Moon has been a source of wonder for humanity for millennia. Its surface is covered in craters, ancient lava plains (called "maria"), and towering highlands. The Moon's gravitational pull drives Earth's tides and stabilizes our planet's axial tilt.

Diameter: 3,474 km
Distance from Earth: 384,400 km
Orbital Period: 27.3 days (0.075 yr)
Day Length: 27.3 Earth days
Surface Temp: -173 to 127 °C
Orbits completed in given time: 0

Mars

Mars, the Red Planet, gets its colour from iron oxide (rust) covering its surface. It has the tallest mountain in the solar system - Olympus Mons at 21.9 km high - and the largest canyon system, Valles Marineris. Mars is a prime target for exploration because evidence suggests it once had liquid water.

Diameter: 6,792 km
Distance from Sun: 227.9 million km
Orbital Period: 687 days (1.88 yr)
Day Length: 24.6 hours
Moons: 2
Orbits completed in given time: 0

Jupiter

Jupiter is the giant of the solar system - its mass is more than twice that of all other planets combined. Its iconic Great Red Spot is a storm larger than Earth that has been raging for hundreds of years. Jupiter has at least 95 known moons, including Ganymede - the largest moon in the solar system.

Diameter: 142,984 km
Distance from Sun: 778.6 million km
Orbital Period: 11.86 yr
Day Length: 9.9 hours
Moons: 95
Orbits completed in given time: 0

Saturn

Saturn is famous for its stunning ring system - made of billions of ice and rock particles stretching over 280,000 km wide but barely 10 metres thick. It is the least dense planet; in fact, Saturn would float in water. Its moon Titan has a thick atmosphere and liquid methane lakes, making it one of the most Earth-like places in the solar system.

Diameter: 120,536 km
Distance from Sun: 1.43 billion km
Orbital Period: 29.46 yr
Day Length: 10.7 hours
Moons: 146
Orbits completed in given time: 0

Uranus

Uranus is an ice giant that spins on its side - its axial tilt of 98° means it essentially rolls around the Sun. Its blue-green colour comes from methane gas in its atmosphere absorbing red light. Uranus has 27 known moons, all named after characters from Shakespeare and Alexander Pope.

Diameter: 51,118 km
Distance from Sun: 2.87 billion km
Orbital Period: 84.01 yr
Day Length: 17.2 hours
Moons: 27
Orbits completed in given time: 0

Neptune

Neptune is the windiest planet in the solar system - gusts can reach 2,100 km/h, more than ten times faster than the strongest hurricanes on Earth. This deep blue ice giant was the first planet located through mathematical prediction rather than observation. Neptune takes 164.8 Earth years to orbit the Sun once.

Diameter: 49,528 km
Distance from Sun: 4.50 billion km
Orbital Period: 164.8 yr
Day Length: 16.1 hours
Moons: 16
Orbits completed in given time: 0
